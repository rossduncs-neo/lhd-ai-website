Introduction
We don't decide rationally.
We decide predictably.
Behavioural economics explains the gap between how we think we decide and how we actually do.
Chapter 00
The old model
Classical economics assumed rational actors
Traditional economics assumed humans weigh costs and benefits, process information objectively, and make decisions that maximise their self-interest. Meet Homo economicus — rational, consistent, and entirely fictional.

"Man is a rational animal — so at least I have been told."
— Bertrand Russell
The new model
Behavioural economics maps how humans actually decide
Humans use mental shortcuts called heuristics. They are swayed by emotion, context, and framing. They make consistent, predictable errors called cognitive biases.

Behavioural economics — pioneered by Kahneman, Tversky, and Thaler — maps these patterns. Once you see them, you can design around them, exploit them ethically, or avoid them in yourself.

Predictably irrational · Systematically wrong · Correctable
The founding idea
1
System 1 — Fast
Automatic. Intuitive. Emotional.
Runs constantly in the background. Jumps to conclusions using pattern recognition. Prone to systematic errors but extraordinarily efficient. This is your brain on autopilot — which is most of the time.
Examples: Reading a face · Driving a familiar route · Snap judgements about people · Recoiling from a loud noise
2
System 2 — Slow
Deliberate. Effortful. Logical.
Lazy by nature — it only engages when System 1 gets stuck. Consumes significant energy. This is your brain actually thinking — which happens far less often than you believe.
Examples: Solving a maths problem · Comparing mortgage rates · Parallel parking · Learning a new skill

The stakes
In everyday life
We save too little for retirement — even when we genuinely intend to save more
We buy things we don't need because they are "on sale"
Doctors and judges make measurably worse decisions when tired or hungry
Investors panic-sell at exactly the wrong moment — every cycle
Voters choose candidates based on name order on the ballot
The opportunity
Once you see the pattern, you can design around it
The insight of behavioural economics is not that humans are stupid. It is that our errors are predictable and systematic — which means they can be anticipated, designed around, and corrected.

Understanding these patterns lets you build better products, make smarter personal decisions, and design environments where people naturally do what is good for them.
The pioneers
Founders

Founded the field together. Developed Prospect Theory. Kahneman received the Nobel Prize in 2002. Tversky died in 1996 — Nobel Prizes are not awarded posthumously.
Nobel 2017

Developed nudge theory and choice architecture. Co-authored "Nudge" with Cass Sunstein. Created the SMarT pension programme.
Populariser

Author of "Predictably Irrational." Made behavioural economics accessible through vivid experiments and storytelling. Professor at Duke.
Influence

Chapter 01
The biases that run your life
16 cognitive biases across 4 categories. Click any card to reveal the real-world example.
16 biases · 4 categories
Perception & Framing
Anchoring Bias
The first number you hear disproportionately influences all subsequent judgements — even when it's irrelevant.
See example
Real-world example
A $999 jacket makes the $350 one feel reasonable. Salary negotiations are won by whoever names a number first. The first price you see sets the "anchor" for everything that follows.
Framing Effect
Identical information presented differently leads to different decisions. Wording changes choices as powerfully as the facts.
See example
Real-world example
"90% fat free" feels healthier than "10% fat." A "$5 credit card surcharge" feels worse than losing a "$5 cash discount." Same reality, different frames, different decisions.
Availability Heuristic
We judge the likelihood of events by how easily examples come to mind — not by actual statistics.
See example
Real-world example
After shark attack news, people fear sharks more than car accidents — despite cars being far more deadly by orders of magnitude. Vivid and recent events distort our sense of probability.
Representativeness
We judge probability by how much something resembles our mental prototype, ignoring base rates entirely.
See example
Real-world example
We assume a quiet, bookish person is more likely a librarian than a truck driver — ignoring that truck drivers vastly outnumber librarians. The stereotype overrides the base rate.
Losses & Gains
Loss Aversion
Losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. We work harder to avoid losing than to win the same amount.
See example
Real-world example
Most people refuse a 50/50 bet to win $150 or lose $100 — even though the expected value is positive. Losing $100 feels worse than winning $150 feels good. Loss aversion is the engine of most bad investment decisions.
Sunk Cost Fallacy
We continue investing because of what we've already spent — not because of future value.
See example
Real-world example
Staying in a bad film because you paid for the ticket. Holding a crashing stock because of what you originally paid. Staying in a bad relationship because of the years already invested.
Endowment Effect
We overvalue things we already own simply because we own them. Ownership creates irrational attachment.
See example
Real-world example
People demand roughly twice as much to sell a mug they own as they would pay to buy that exact same mug. Ownership inflates perceived value dramatically and irrationally.
Prospect Theory
We value gains and losses relative to a reference point — not in absolute terms. Diminishing sensitivity applies to both.
See example
Real-world example
A raise from $50k to $55k feels better than one from $95k to $100k — both are $5k, but the first is proportionally much larger from the reference point.
Social & Self
Social Proof
When uncertain, we look to what others are doing and use it as evidence for the correct behaviour.
See example
Real-world example
"Most guests in this hotel reuse their towels" gets significantly more towel reuse than "please save the environment." Telling people what others do is more powerful than moral appeals.
Confirmation Bias
We seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms what we already believe.
See example
Real-world example
After buying a car you suddenly notice the same model everywhere and feel validated. Before buying, you only read positive reviews and dismissed the negative ones without examining them.
Optimism Bias
We overestimate good outcomes for ourselves and underestimate bad ones. Most people believe they're above average at almost everything.
See example
Real-world example
80% of drivers rate themselves in the top 30%. Most projects run over budget and over time — in every industry, in every country, in every decade. Optimism bias is universal.
Hindsight Bias
After an event occurs, we believe we knew the outcome all along. This prevents honest learning.
See example
Real-world example
"I always knew that startup would fail." You invested in it. "I knew the market would crash." You didn't sell before it did. Hindsight rewrites memory to feel like foresight.
Time & Choice
Present Bias
We dramatically overweight immediate rewards compared to future ones. The future self is an abstract stranger.
See example
Real-world example
You know you should go to the gym tomorrow, save for retirement next month, start the diet next week — every week, forever. The future you feels unreal. Only now feels real.
Status Quo Bias
We strongly prefer things to remain as they are. Change requires effort; inertia is the path of least resistance.
See example
Real-world example
Most people never change their energy supplier, pension fund, or insurance — even when materially better options are available and easy to access. The default wins by default.
Choice Overload
Too many options leads to decision paralysis and often no decision at all. Abundance paradoxically reduces choosing.
See example
Real-world example
A supermarket offering 24 jam varieties sold far less than one offering 6 — despite the larger range appearing more appealing at first glance. More choice is not always better.
Halo Effect
One positive trait colours our entire perception of a person or product. A single quality contaminates all other assessments.
See example
Real-world example
Attractive people are automatically perceived as smarter, kinder, and more competent. Apple's iPod halo sold Macs. One great product made every Apple product seem better than it was.
Chapter 02
8 studies that changed everything
8 landmark experiments. Click each to expand the full findings.
8 experiments
01
Iyengar & Lepper, 2000 — Columbia
The Jam Study — Choice Overload
What they did
Set up two supermarket tasting stations — one with 24 flavours of jam, one with 6. Measured how many people stopped, tasted, and bought. The larger display attracted more visitors. The smaller display sold far more.
What it proved
The 6-jam display converted buyers at 10x the rate of the 24-jam display. More choice leads to less action. Choice overload is real, measurable, and costly in business — and in life.
Choice overloadFrictionDecision paralysis
02
Güth, Schmittberger & Schwarze, 1982
The Ultimatum Game — Fairness over Logic
What they did
Player A is given $100 and can offer any split to Player B. Player B can accept (both get the money) or reject (both get nothing). Rational players should accept any offer — any money beats nothing.
What it proved
Offers below 30% were regularly rejected. People would rather lose money than accept an unfair deal. Fairness overrides self-interest. Humans are not the rational agents classical economics assumed.
FairnessLoss aversionSocial norms
03
Johnson & Goldstein, 2003
The Organ Donor Default — Default Power
What they did
Compared organ donation rates across European countries with "opt-in" vs "opt-out" systems. Same populations. Different defaults. Opt-in: you must sign up. Opt-out: you are enrolled unless you actively refuse.
What it proved
Opt-in countries: ~12% participation. Opt-out countries: ~97% participation. The default — not personal values — determines life-or-death decisions for millions. This is the foundational nudge study.
Status quo biasDefault powerNudge theory
04
Kahneman & Tversky, 1979
The Asian Disease Problem — Framing
What they did
Participants chose between two treatments for a disease that would kill 600. Framing A: "saves 200 lives" vs "⅓ chance of saving all, ⅔ chance of saving none." Framing B: "400 die" vs "⅓ chance no one dies, ⅔ chance 600 die."
What it proved
When framed as lives saved, participants chose certainty. When framed as lives lost, they became risk-seeking. Identical outcomes. Opposite decisions. Framing is one of the most powerful forces in all of decision-making.
FramingLoss aversionRisk perception
05
Ariely, Loewenstein & Prelec, 2003 — MIT
Social Security Anchoring
What they did
Students wrote down the last two digits of their Social Security number, then bid on items including wine and chocolates. They were first asked whether they would pay that amount, then their maximum price.
What it proved
Students with high SSN endings bid 60–120% more than those with low endings. A completely random, irrelevant number determined willingness to pay. Anchoring affects even experts on their home turf.
AnchoringArbitrary coherencePricing
06
Thaler & Johnson, 1990
Mental Accounting — Money Isn't Fungible
What they did
Participants were given hypothetical scenarios involving money won at a casino vs money earned. They were asked how they would spend it, and how freely. The source of the money was varied while the amounts were held constant.
What it proved
People spent "found" money far more freely than earned money, despite identical amounts. We treat money differently based on its source. Financial decisions are deeply irrational — and predictably so.
Mental accountingHouse moneyWindfall
07
HMRC Behavioural Insights Team, 2012
Tax Letters & Social Norms
What they did
Tested different versions of HMRC tax reminder letters. The control was a standard reminder. Test letters added one sentence: "9 out of 10 people in your area pay their tax on time." Sent to millions of late payers.
What it proved
The social norm letter significantly increased on-time payments. The intervention cost essentially nothing. Generated hundreds of millions in earlier tax collection. One sentence beat elaborate financial incentives.
Social normsNudgePolicy design
08
Milgram, 1963 — Yale
Authority Obedience — Context Overrides Character
What they did
Participants were told to deliver electric shocks to a "learner" when they gave wrong answers. An authority figure in a lab coat instructed them to continue. The shocks were fake; the "learner" was an actor.
What it proved
65% of participants delivered what they believed were dangerous shocks when instructed by authority. Situation and context override personal character and values far more than we believe. This is the most important finding in all of social psychology.
Authority biasSituation vs characterSocial compliance

Putting it to work
Practical applications across three domains. Select a tab to explore.
3 domains · 15 applications
01
Design your environment before the decision
Put healthy food at eye level, remove junk from visible spaces. Keep your phone out of the bedroom. Place your gym bag by the door. Your environment makes most of your decisions before you consciously engage.
Biases used: Status quo bias · Friction · Defaults
02
Use commitment devices for important habits
Lock in future good behaviour before temptation arrives. Automate savings on payday. Book gym sessions in advance. Write your goals publicly. Your rational present self constrains your irrational future self.
Biases used: Present bias · Commitment & consistency
03
Apply the 48-hour cooling-off rule to purchases
Before any significant purchase, wait 48 hours. The emotional drive fades reliably. The rational assessment becomes clearer without effort. Most impulse purchases are abandoned after a cooling period.
Biases used: Present bias · Emotional override · Loss aversion
04
Use the 10-10-10 rule for important decisions
Ask: how will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? Most good decisions look stable across timescales. Most impulsive ones only look good in the 10-minute window.
Biases used: Present bias · Temporal discounting
05
Audit your default settings quarterly
Review your pension fund, phone notifications, subscription defaults, and supermarket habits. Who designed these defaults? Not you. Someone with different incentives. Reclaim intentional control annually.
Biases used: Status quo bias · Inertia
01
Make the right choice the default
Default to the option you want customers to choose. Pre-tick the better insurance tier. Make the more sustainable shipping option the default. Opt-out vastly outperforms opt-in for almost every behaviour.
Biases used: Status quo bias · Default power · Inertia
02
Use social proof precisely and specifically
"Most popular" labels drive sales. "9 out of 10 customers choose" outperforms vague endorsements. The more specific and geographically relevant the social proof, the stronger its effect.
Biases used: Social proof · Uncertainty · Conformity
03
Reduce choices at the decision point
Three pricing tiers outperform seven. A recommended option reduces decision fatigue. Paradox of choice is real — fewer options drive more conversions. Guide the customer rather than overwhelming them.
Biases used: Choice overload · Decision fatigue
04
Anchor high, then discount
Set the reference price high and prominently visible. Crossed-out original prices work because the higher number anchors perceived value. Anchoring shapes willingness to pay more than any other pricing lever.
Biases used: Anchoring · Reference points · Loss aversion
05
Frame losses, not gains, for high-priority actions
"Don't miss out on $200 in savings" outperforms "Earn $200 in savings." Losses motivate more than equivalent gains in almost all contexts. Loss-framed messages drive urgency more effectively.
Biases used: Loss aversion · Framing · Prospect theory
01
Automate everything to defeat present bias
Direct debit your savings on payday before you see the money. Auto-invest into index funds monthly. Once automated, you never have to make the virtuous decision again — and can never sabotage it impulsively.
Biases used: Present bias · Inertia · Mental accounting
02
Create a decision journal for trades and investments
Before entering any position, write: what am I buying, why, what price would prove me wrong, when will I review. Read it back later. The gap between your predictions and reality will be humbling and instructive.
Biases used: Hindsight bias · Overconfidence · Confirmation bias
03
Use the "would I buy this today?" test
When evaluating any holding, ask: if I had cash and no position, would I buy this at this price today? If no, sell. Ignore what you paid. The sunk cost is irrelevant. Only the future matters.
Biases used: Sunk cost · Endowment effect · Loss aversion
04
Dollar-cost average to remove timing decisions
Invest fixed amounts regularly regardless of market conditions. Removes the "perfect time to buy" fallacy entirely. Counters market-timing hubris. Systematically buys more when prices are low and less when high.
Biases used: Overconfidence · Anchoring · Timing illusion
05
Treat all money identically regardless of source
A tax refund, bonus, or windfall is the same as earned income. Before spending any windfall, apply the same test you would apply to core salary. Mental accounting leads to predictably poor windfall decisions.
Biases used: Mental accounting · House money effect
Chapter 04
Traps you'll fall into anyway
7 common mistakes — each with a concrete fix. Click to expand.
7 mistakes · with fixes
01
Thinking you're immune to bias
The trap
The "bias blind spot" — believing you are less biased than average — is itself a cognitive bias. Studies consistently show that even people who have just been taught about a specific bias immediately exhibit it. Knowledge alone is not protection.
The fix
Build systems and processes that assume bias. Do not rely on willpower or self-awareness in the moment. Design the decision environment instead of relying on your character to override it.
02
Holding losing investments too long
The trap
The endowment effect combined with sunk cost fallacy creates investors who cling to terrible positions. The purchase price is completely irrelevant to future performance. The market does not care what you paid.
The fix
Ask: "Would I buy this today at this price?" If the honest answer is no, sell. Ignore what you paid. The past is gone. Only the future counts. Run this test on every holding quarterly.
03
Overconfidence in your own forecasts
The trap
90% confidence intervals from experts contain the right answer only 50% of the time. We are systematically overconfident — particularly in domains where we have some expertise. The more you know, the more confident you become. Often incorrectly.
The fix
Widen your confidence intervals. Actively seek out people who disagree and engage with their arguments seriously rather than dismissing them. Keep a forecast log and check your actual hit rate.
04
Making decisions immediately after consuming news
The trap
News is optimised for availability heuristic triggers. Plane crashes, murders, and disasters are vastly overrepresented. Your risk model gets systematically distorted every time you read the news then make a decision.
The fix
Separate information-gathering from decision-making. Never make financial or major life decisions immediately after consuming news media. Create a buffer of at least 24 hours between reading and deciding.
05
Treating a good outcome as proof of a good decision
The trap
You can make a terrible decision and get lucky. Hindsight bias then convinces you it was brilliant. Poker players call it "resulting" — judging process quality by outcome quality. This kills honest learning and improves nothing.
The fix
Judge decisions by the process and information available at the time — not the result. Keep a decision journal. Review decisions and outcomes separately, weeks or months apart.
06
Spending windfalls differently than earned income
The trap
Mental accounting leads people to spend a tax refund on luxuries but carefully guard the same amount of earned income. The casino effect — gambling winnings feel "free" — bleeds into everyday financial life. Money is money.
The fix
Treat all money identically regardless of source. Before spending any windfall, apply the same decision test you would to core salary. Delay all windfall decisions by at least 48 hours.
07
Not designing your decision environment
The trap
Most people try to overcome biases through willpower at the exact moment of decision. This almost never works sustainably. The cafeteria layout matters more than nutrition education. Environment beats intention, every time.
The fix
Invest effort before, not during. Change defaults, remove friction from good choices, add friction to bad ones. Design your environment deliberately — don't rely on resisting it with willpower you won't have.
Chapter 05
10 strategies. Use them today.
Engineered interventions — practical tools that work with your biases, not against them.
10 tools
Tool 01
Implementation Intentions
Transform vague intentions ("I'll exercise more") into specific if-then plans. Bridges the intention-action gap and removes in-the-moment decision fatigue. The plan decides for you before temptation arrives.
Write "When [situation X], I will do [behaviour Y]." E.g., "When I sit at my desk Monday morning, I will write for 30 minutes before checking email." Specificity is everything.
Tool 02
The 10-10-10 Rule
Counter present bias by asking three questions before any significant decision: how will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? Most good decisions look similar across timescales.
Write down your answers honestly. Decisions that look very different across timescales deserve more scrutiny. Especially useful for emotional decisions made under pressure.
Tool 03
The Decision Journal
Before important decisions, write down what you're deciding, why, what you predict will happen, and how confident you are. Review past entries regularly. The most powerful calibration tool available.
Takes 5 minutes per decision. After 3–6 months you will have a clear picture of your actual accuracy versus perceived accuracy. The gap is always humbling. And always useful.
Tool 04
Commitment Devices
Use your rational present self to constrain your irrational future self. Lock in good behaviour before temptation arrives. The key is that the commitment must be binding — mere intentions do not work.
Auto-transfer savings on payday. Book morning gym sessions in advance. Delete social media apps and reinstall only during scheduled windows. Make the good choice the automatic choice.
Tool 05
Consider the Opposite
The single most effective debiasing technique for confirmation bias. Actively generate the strongest possible case against your current position. Assign a devil's advocate for team decisions.
For any belief or plan, spend 10 minutes writing the best argument against it. In meetings, assign someone to formally argue the opposite. Research shows this alone significantly improves forecast accuracy.
Tool 06
Reference Class Forecasting
Instead of asking "how long will this take?" ask "how long do projects like this actually take?" Base your estimate on the outside view — not your optimistic inside view.
Before estimating time or cost for any project, research completion rates and average overruns for comparable projects. Then adjust upward. The answer will feel wrong. It will be more accurate than your estimate.
Tool 07
Friction Engineering
Add steps to bad habits, remove steps from good ones. The size of the barrier matters more than motivation. One extra step can halve a bad behaviour. One fewer step can double a good one.
Keep healthy food at eye level, junk food out of sight. Log out of social media. Put your phone charger in another room at night. One fewer click to save. One more click to spend.
Tool 08
The Premortem
Before launching a project, imagine the failure has already happened. Work backwards to diagnose what went wrong. Forces surfacing of risks that optimism bias suppresses in forward-looking planning.
"It is 18 months from now. The project failed completely. Write down why." Give each person time to write independently before discussing. Developed by Gary Klein. Used by the best teams in every field.
Tool 09
Cooling-Off Periods
Deliberately create time between impulse and action for any significant decision. The emotional drive fades reliably. The rational assessment becomes clearer without effort. Time is the cheapest debiasing tool available.
24 hours for purchases over a set threshold. One week for larger amounts. Remove items from wishlists rather than carts — see if you add them back. Most of the time, you won't.
Tool 10
Choice Architecture Audit
Regularly review the environment around your most important decisions. Who designed your defaults? Not you — someone with different interests. Reclaim control over the architecture of your own choices.
Check your pension default fund, phone notifications, supermarket route, browser homepage, and app layouts. Redesign each intentionally. Do this annually — your environment changes, and so should your architecture.

6 real cases where understanding bias created extraordinary results.
6 cases
Netflix
Default autoplay & choice reduction
Status quo
Choice overload
Present bias
The behaviour
Netflix auto-plays the next episode after a short countdown, inverting the default from "choose to continue" to "choose to stop." Its algorithm also narrows a library of 6,000 titles to a curated slate of ~40, solving choice overload at the point of maximum indecision.
The result
The countdown creates soft friction against quitting. The 10-second window is long enough to feel like a choice, short enough to almost never be exercised. Now an industry standard. Reduced churn by keeping users past the natural "shall I stop?" decision point.
Spotify
Social proof & personalisation anchoring
Anchoring
Social proof
Identity
The behaviour
Spotify Wrapped uses your own listening data as an anchor ("you listened to this artist for 47 hours") that feels significant regardless of objective meaning. The specificity of the numbers makes the data feel personal and share-worthy. It triggers identity signalling through social sharing.
The result
Generated hundreds of millions of organic social media posts annually at near-zero cost. Turned user data review into the largest annual social media event in music. Drives resubscriptions at year-end. The most cost-efficient marketing event in the industry.
Amazon
1-Click, Prime & friction elimination
Sunk cost
Friction
Anchoring
The behaviour
Amazon's entire commerce system is engineered around friction elimination. 1-Click removed every checkout barrier. Prime converts shipping cost from a per-decision variable to a sunk cost — once you've paid the annual fee, you buy more to "get your money's worth."
The result
Prime members spend roughly 4x more annually than non-Prime members. Friction reduction directly increases impulse purchase rates. Over 200 million Prime members worldwide. Prime is the backbone of Amazon's retail dominance — built entirely on behavioural principles.
UK Nudge Unit
Tax letters & social norms
Social proof
Norms
Loss aversion
The behaviour
The UK's Behavioural Insights Team tested different versions of HMRC tax reminder letters. Adding one sentence — telling recipients that most people in their area pay their tax on time — increased on-time payments measurably at essentially zero cost.
The result
Generated hundreds of millions in earlier tax collection. The Nudge Unit's interventions have created over $1 billion in estimated value for the UK government. Now replicated by 200+ governments worldwide. The highest return-on-investment government programme in modern history.
SMarT Plan
Pension savings through commitment design
Present bias
Loss aversion
Status quo
The behaviour
Thaler and Benartzi's trial let workers commit to increasing pension contributions with future pay rises — not now. This sidesteps present bias (no immediate sacrifice), loss aversion (never feels like a pay cut), and inertia (auto-enrolled). Three biases, one elegant design.
The result
Savings rates went from 3.5% to 13.6% over 40 months without compulsion. Now the structural basis for pension auto-enrolment in many countries. UK auto-enrolment has added tens of billions to pension savings since 2012.
Duolingo
Streaks, loss aversion & commitment loops
Loss aversion
Endowment
Commitment
The behaviour
Duolingo's streak mechanic is pure loss aversion in product form. Once you have a 30-day streak, the prospect of losing it becomes more motivating than the desire to learn. They even offer "streak freezes" — because protecting a streak is more powerful than building a habit from scratch.
The result
Users with active streaks show dramatically higher retention than those without. The streak is cited as the app's single most important retention mechanic. 500M+ users. $5B+ valuation. Built largely on behavioural mechanics rather than language-learning quality alone.

Test your understanding
5 questions to find out which biases you're most susceptible to.
5 questions
Self-Assessment Quiz
Read each scenario carefully. Choose the answer that describes your instinctive reaction — not the "correct" one.
Question 1 of 5
You've been watching a film for 90 minutes and you're not enjoying it. You paid $20 for the ticket. What do you do?
Stay and watch it to the end — you've already paid
Leave immediately — the ticket price is gone either way
Stay a bit longer to see if it gets better, then decide
✓ Correct. The $20 is a sunk cost — it's gone whether you stay or leave. The only relevant question is: will the next 30 minutes be worth your time? The ticket price is irrelevant.
This is Sunk Cost Fallacy. The $20 is already spent — staying doesn't recover it. The only question is whether the remaining time is worth the investment. Past costs should never drive future decisions.
Question 2 of 5
A doctor says a surgery has a "90% survival rate." Another says it has a "10% mortality rate." How do you feel about each?
The first option sounds much safer and more reassuring
They're identical statements — I'd feel the same about both
The second option sounds riskier and more worrying
✓ Correct. These are mathematically identical statements. Most people instinctively prefer the "90% survival" framing, which demonstrates how powerfully framing alters our perception of identical information.
This is the Framing Effect. "90% survival rate" and "10% mortality rate" are mathematically identical. Yet most people perceive one as safer than the other. Framing changes decisions as powerfully as the underlying facts.
Question 3 of 5
A 50/50 coin flip: heads you win $150, tails you lose $100. The expected value is +$25. Do you take the bet?
Yes — the expected value is positive, so I take it
No — losing $100 feels too bad to risk it
Probably not — it depends on my mood
✓ The rational answer. Most people refuse this bet — they feel the potential loss of $100 more acutely than the potential gain of $150. This is loss aversion at work. Losses hurt approximately twice as much as equivalent gains feel good.
This is Loss Aversion — one of the most documented biases in all of economics. Losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good, causing us to refuse bets with positive expected value. The rational answer is to take it.
Question 4 of 5
You receive a $2,000 tax refund. How are you likely to treat this money compared to your regular monthly salary?
Spend it more freely — it feels like a bonus or found money
Exactly the same — it's the same money regardless of source
Save it more carefully — it feels harder to replace than a salary
✓ Correct. All money is fungible — $2,000 is $2,000 regardless of how it arrived. Most people treat windfalls as "free money" and spend them far more freely than equivalent earned income, which is mental accounting in action.
This is Mental Accounting. People spend "found" money far more freely than earned money, despite identical amounts. A tax refund is simply money that was always yours — yet it activates completely different spending psychology. Money is money.
Question 5 of 5
A new restaurant has opened. You see a long queue outside. You weren't planning to eat there, but you're curious. What do you think?
It must be excellent — so many people can't be wrong
The queue is interesting evidence but I'd want to know why before deciding
I'd probably join the queue — missing something popular feels bad
✓ Good critical thinking. The queue could mean the food is excellent — or that it's new and hype-driven, or they're slow, or it's cheap. Social proof is real and powerful but should be examined, not followed blindly.
This is Social Proof. When uncertain, we look to what others are doing as evidence for the correct behaviour. A queue signals quality — but it could also signal newness, hype, or slow service. The crowd is not always right.

First principle
Design, don't resist
Willpower is finite and unreliable. Build systems and environments that make good decisions the path of least resistance. The choice architecture matters more than the motivation to make a good choice.
Second principle
Slow down before big decisions
System 1 is fast and often wrong. System 2 is slow and more accurate. The single most powerful intervention for important choices is creating time between impulse and action. Time is free. Use it.
Third principle
Assume you're biased
Don't try to detect bias in yourself in the moment — it doesn't work. Use processes that are immune to your biases: journals, pre-mortems, devil's advocates. Systems beat self-awareness.
