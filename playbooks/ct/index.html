The Preface
Complexity has a hidden tax.
00
The Complexity Tax
If nobody sees it — nobody reviews it.

It isn't line-itemed in a budget review. No auditor flags it. No consultant has ever been hired to find it — because finding it would require admitting it's there. It compounds quietly. In slowed decisions. In duplicated effort. In meetings that exist to manage the output of other meetings.

Nobody builds complexity on purpose. It arrives one sensible decision at a time. Then one day the layers are the work.

This playbook has two readers
The one who wants to fix the organisation. And the one who quietly suspects they might be part of what needs fixing.
Both are welcome here.
Chapter 01
The tax nobody sends a bill for
Every organisation pays it. Almost none of them know it exists.
The Bill
Where it comes from
It is not the result of bad leadership.

It is the entirely predictable consequence of human psychology operating inside organisational systems. Organisations are not rational entities. They are human beings at scale — and every bias that distorts individual decision-making distorts organisational decision-making too. With one critical difference.

When an individual acts irrationally, the consequences are personal. When an organisation does, the behaviour gets a budget. A team. A job title. And eventually, a culture.

The Mechanism
Biases become institutional — and invisible.

When an organisation acts on the same bias repeatedly, it stops looking like a bias. It looks like process. It gets defended in quarterly reviews. It gets inherited by new hires who never question it because nobody told them there was anything to question.

Good leaders don't design against this. Most don't even see it. The seven chapters that follow identify the seven biases that levy the tax — and what to do about each one.

Who Creates It
They meant well. They always do.

There is always someone in the room who called the meeting to align on the meeting. Who added the approval step — just to be safe. Who kept the report nobody reads. Who said yes to the new tool without asking what it would replace.

This is The Accumulator.

Not a villain. Not a bad leader. A person responding — entirely rationally — to an environment that rewards addition and makes subtraction invisible. In some organisations, The Accumulator is the culture.

Chapter 02
The Preservation Problem
Organisational status quo bias — why nothing ever gets removed.
Status quo bias
02
The individual bias
Status Quo Bias
People have a strong, irrational preference for the current state of affairs — even when changing it would make them objectively better off. The default option wins not because it is the best option, but because it is already there.
The organisational pattern
The museum of past decisions
Processes get added — rarely removed. Tools get adopted — almost never retired. Meetings get scheduled — almost never cancelled. Every addition feels like progress. Every removal feels like risk. The result is an organisation carrying the full weight of every reasonable decision anyone has ever made inside it.
The company in the mirror
General Electric
Under Jeff Immelt, GE added businesses, initiatives, and management layers throughout the 2000s and early 2010s. By 2017, the company had become so complex that analysts struggled to value it and leadership struggled to manage it. The additions had been celebrated. The cost was only visible in the subtraction — which took years and destroyed billions in market value.
The diagnostic question
"What would we stop doing tomorrow if we were building this function from scratch today?"
If you cannot answer this question quickly, the Preservation Problem is active. The processes you cannot name for removal are the ones doing the most damage.
The LESS intervention
Replace the optimisation list with a kill list. For every new process, tool, or initiative approved, one existing thing must be removed — not improved, not optimised, removed. Schedule a quarterly subtraction review with the same seniority as a budget review.
L — Lighten E — Eliminate
The structural cause

This is not a character flaw. It is a structural consequence of how organisations measure and reward activity. Output metrics track what gets added. Almost nothing tracks what gets removed. Addition is rewarded. Subtraction is invisible at best, risky at worst. The organisation that removes as readily as it adds is fundamentally faster. The one that only adds is not growing. It is accumulating.

Chapter 03
Strategic Paralysis
Organisational loss aversion — why the cost of inaction is never calculated.
Loss aversion
03
The individual bias
Loss Aversion
Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains in human psychology. We work harder to avoid losing than to win the same amount. Applied to organisational strategy, this asymmetry is consistently and expensively destructive.
The organisational pattern
The inability to cannibalise
Loss aversion manifests as the inability to cut, cannibalise, or fundamentally change anything that currently generates revenue — even when the strategic case is overwhelming. The existing business feels real, tangible, defensible. The new direction feels speculative. And so organisations protect what they have until the moment they can't.
The company in the mirror
Kodak
Kodak invented the digital camera in 1975. The technology that would eventually destroy the company existed inside the company for decades. But film was generating enormous margins, and digital threatened those margins directly. Loss aversion won every meeting for twenty years. Kodak filed for bankruptcy in 2012. The loss they were trying to avoid became the loss they could not escape.
The diagnostic question
"What is the cost of not making this change, compounded over three years?"
Most organisations only calculate one number: what the change costs. Loss aversion is neutralised when you make the cost of inaction equally visible. Both are real numbers. Make both appear on the same page.
The LESS intervention
Reframe the strategic question entirely. Stop asking "what does this cost us?" Start asking "what is the cost of not doing this?" Make inaction as expensive on paper as it is in reality. The number that isn't calculated is always the one that matters most.
S — Sharpen
The warning sign

When the language of strategy becomes the language of defence — "protecting," "preserving," "maintaining" — loss aversion is in the room. This is not timidity. It is loss aversion operating at board level, dressed in the language of prudence and fiduciary responsibility. The framing feels responsible. The outcome rarely is.

Chapter 04
Competitive Copying
Organisational social proof — when strategy becomes a queue.
Social proof
04
The individual bias
Social Proof
When uncertain, we look to what others are doing and treat it as evidence of what we should do. It is a useful shortcut in genuinely uncertain situations. In organisational strategy, it is a substitute for thinking.
The organisational pattern
Strategy as a queue
Competitive copying is social proof industrialised. An organisation sees a competitor launch an initiative, restructure a function, or adopt a tool. The rational response would be to evaluate whether it makes sense for them. The actual response is to replicate it — because replication feels safe, and originality feels exposed. The result is a market where everyone does the same things, pursuing the same metrics, making genuine differentiation almost impossible.
The company in the mirror
Twitter / X
Between 2020 and 2022, Twitter launched Stories (Fleets), Spaces, newsletters, Super Follows, Communities, and a subscription tier — each in direct response to a competitor. None became defining products. Meanwhile, the core timeline — Twitter's actual differentiator — deteriorated. Competitive copying produced a product without a clear identity, serving nobody particularly well.
The diagnostic question
"Are we doing this because it is right for us, or because others are doing it?"
These are not the same question. Conflating them is precisely how competitive copying masquerades as competitive strategy. If the honest answer is "because others are doing it" — that is not a strategy. That is a queue.
The LESS intervention
Before adopting any initiative, tool, or structural change, require an explicit written answer to the diagnostic question above. Make it a mandatory agenda item for any proposal that references a competitor. The organisations that ask it consistently make better decisions than those that don't.
E — Eliminate S — Simplify
The MarTech example

The average enterprise marketing technology stack now exceeds 90 tools. The average CMO, asked to justify each one individually, cannot. They were adopted because others were adopting them. They remain because removal requires a justification that adoption never did. Competitive copying at industrial scale. The 9,304% growth in MarTech tools between 2011 and 2024 is not a technology story. It is a social proof story.

Chapter 05
The Investment Trap
Organisational sunk cost bias — why failing projects keep getting funded.
Sunk cost bias
05
The individual bias
Sunk Cost Fallacy
We continue investing in failing endeavours because of what we have already spent, rather than evaluating the future on its own terms. Past investment is irrelevant to future value. Rationally, everyone knows this. Behaviourally, almost nobody acts on it.
The organisational pattern
Three compounding sunk costs
In organisations, sunk cost operates across three dimensions simultaneously. Financial: projects continue receiving funding because of what has already been invested. Reputational: initiatives championed by senior leaders cannot be killed without the champion admitting failure. Structural: entire functions built around a strategy that has since changed continue to exist because reorganising them is complicated.
The company in the mirror
Quibi
Quibi raised $1.75 billion to launch a short-form mobile video platform in 2020. Within six months, the evidence that the product was not working was extensive and available to everyone involved. The platform shut down after seven months. The warning signs had been visible early. But $1.75 billion creates an almost irresistible pull toward continuation. The sunk cost owned the decision.
The diagnostic question
"Would we fund this today, knowing only what we know about the future?"
Not "given what we've spent, should we continue?" That question is contaminated by the past. The only strategically relevant question is the forward-looking one. Build it into every portfolio review. Make it required, not optional.
The LESS intervention
One question. Required. Non-negotiable: "Would we fund this today?" Build it into every portfolio review as a mandatory agenda item. The organisations that ask it consistently make better decisions than those that don't. The gap compounds. Separate the decision from the history.
E — Eliminate S — Sharpen
The reputational trap

The most dangerous form of organisational sunk cost is not financial. It is reputational. Initiatives personally championed by senior leaders are almost impossible to kill — because terminating them requires the champion to publicly acknowledge a failed judgement. The project continues not because it creates value, but because stopping it would create accountability. Design the review process to remove the person from the decision.

Chapter 06
The Quarter Horizon
Organisational present bias — why simplification never gets funded.
Present bias
06
The individual bias
Present Bias
We dramatically overweight immediate rewards compared to future ones. The future self is an abstract stranger. The current quarter is real, measurable, and what you will be evaluated on in approximately eleven weeks.
The organisational pattern
The unfunded simplification programme
Simplification programmes almost never get funded — because their benefits are diffuse, long-term, and hard to attribute to any single decision. The cost of complexity is invisible. The cost of the programme itself is immediate and specific. Brand investment is sacrificed to performance marketing. Technology infrastructure is chronically underfunded. The quarter horizon is not irresponsibility. It is the rational response to an incentive structure that measures short-term performance.
The company in the mirror
WeWork
WeWork's relentless prioritisation of short-term growth metrics — locations opened, desks sold, headline revenue — over unit economics created a company that looked exceptional in the present and was structurally unsound in the future. Each quarter, in isolation, the metrics looked acceptable. The compound consequence of fifty consecutive acceptable quarters was a $47bn valuation that collapsed to near-zero within eighteen months.
The diagnostic question
"What are we deferring that will cost us more to fix in two years than it would to address now?"
The cost of deferral is real but invisible. Making it visible is the intervention. Calculate it. Put the number in the same meeting as the quarterly performance review.
The LESS intervention
Separate strategic investment from quarterly performance review — structurally, not just rhetorically. Ring-fence capital for investments whose returns will be realised over 18 months or more and protect it from short-term pressure. Make the cost of present bias visible: for any investment being deferred, calculate and present the compounding cost of deferral explicitly.
L — Lighten S — Sharpen
The incentive structure problem

Present bias in organisations is not a leadership character flaw. It is a rational response to an incentive structure that measures and rewards short-term performance. Which means it is, in most large organisations, the rational response to the incentive structures those organisations have built for themselves. You cannot cure present bias with exhortation. You cure it by redesigning the incentive.

Chapter 07
Priority Inflation
Organisational choice overload — when everything is important, nothing is.
Choice overload
07
The individual bias
Choice Overload
Beyond a certain threshold, every additional option reduces the quality of the eventual decision. More choice reliably leads to less action, lower satisfaction, and worse outcomes. The cognitive cost of evaluation overwhelms the benefit of range.
The organisational pattern
The seventeen-priority strategy
Priority inflation is what happens when organisations cannot say no. Every initiative sounds reasonable in isolation. Every stakeholder's request has merit. The result is a strategic plan with seventeen priorities — which is functionally a plan with no priorities. Attention, energy, and capital spread across the full surface area of the list. Nothing receives the concentration required to become genuinely excellent. Breakthroughs become structurally almost impossible.
The company in the mirror
Yahoo
Yahoo's strategic history is a masterclass in priority inflation. At various points, Yahoo was simultaneously pursuing search, email, news, finance, sports, entertainment, social networking, mobile, and display advertising — each with genuine investment and genuine internal champions. The company was never short of priorities. It was chronically short of focus. Google did one thing with extraordinary depth. Yahoo had more priorities. Google had more progress.
The diagnostic question
"If we could only do three of these things, which three would they be?"
Not which three are most urgent. Which three, if we did them excellently, would matter most in three years? The answer to this question is your actual strategy. Everything else is list management.
The LESS intervention
No new priority without removing one. Not as a guideline — as a rule. Treat prioritisation as a subtractive discipline. A strategy with five genuine priorities — properly resourced and actively protected — will outperform a strategy with fifteen nominal ones every time. Less is not a compromise. It is the condition under which excellence becomes possible.
E — Eliminate S — Simplify
Priority inflation in marketing

Priority inflation is particularly acute in marketing functions, where the number of channels, formats, tools, audiences, and metrics available has expanded dramatically while underlying budget and team capacity has not. The CMO managing twelve channels, six agency relationships, forty-plus tools, and a hundred-line dashboard is not running a marketing function. They are administering one. The distinction matters.

Chapter 08
The Legacy Anchor
Organisational anchoring bias — when last year decides this year.
Anchoring bias
08
The individual bias
Anchoring Bias
The first number encountered in any estimation or negotiation exerts a disproportionate influence on all subsequent judgements — even when that number is demonstrably arbitrary. The anchor does not have to be relevant. It just has to be first.
The organisational pattern
Last year as the starting point
In organisations, the anchor is almost always last year. Last year's budget. Last year's headcount. Last year's structure. Last year's targets. Annual planning processes are, at their core, anchoring exercises — negotiations conducted from the reference point of what already exists, rather than from the question of what should exist. The legacy anchor feels responsible. It is, behaviourally, a failure to ask the right question.
The company in the mirror
McKinsey
McKinsey's identity as the definitive global strategy firm has become its own legacy anchor. As the market moved toward embedded, tech-native, outcome-accountable advisory, McKinsey's response has been incremental — new practice areas bolted onto an unchanged model. The anchor is the prestige of the existing brand. Every strategic conversation begins from what McKinsey has always been, not from what the market now requires.
The diagnostic question
"If we were building this from scratch today, knowing what we know, would we build this?"
This is the question that breaks the anchor. It is not zero-based budgeting applied to everything every year — that creates its own complexity. It is the deliberate, periodic refusal to let last year be the starting point for next year's thinking.
The LESS intervention
Every three years, require that significant budget allocations, team structures, and strategic priorities be justified from first principles — not from last year's position. If the honest answer to the diagnostic question is no, the legacy anchor has been deciding on your behalf. Make that visible. Then remove it.

The bill, presented

Seven biases. One tax. None of them require bad leadership to operate. They require only what every organisation already has in abundance: humans, making decisions, under pressure, inside systems that were never designed to resist accumulation.

A note on AI

Humans are no longer making all the decisions. AI is being layered into these same systems. The biases that generate the Complexity Tax are not neutralised by new capability — in many cases they are accelerated by one. AI doesn't get its own chapter here. This is a deliberate choice. AI is the auditor. The bill was always there. Now it has a deadline.

The bill, presented
The Complexity Tax compounds invisibly — until one day the accumulated weight produces an organisation that cannot move at the speed its market demands.

The good news: unlike most taxes, this one is entirely optional.
Chapter 09
Your Complexity Audit
Four questions most organisations have never formally asked themselves.
Self-assessment
A first look at your bill
Most organisations can answer question one.
Almost none can answer question two.
The gap between what you have added in three years and what you have removed in three years is your Complexity Tax. These four questions make it visible. Be honest. The discomfort is the point.
01
What have we added in the last three years?
Processes, tools, meetings, headcount, initiatives, agency relationships, reporting requirements, approval layers. List them. Most organisations can do this easily — the additions are visible and celebrated.
02
What have we removed in the last three years?
Same categories. Processes stopped. Tools retired. Meetings cancelled permanently. Headcount genuinely reduced. Initiatives formally killed. If this list is significantly shorter than question one — and it almost certainly is — that gap is your Complexity Tax.
03
What are we continuing only because it already exists?
Not because it is working. Not because it is strategically important. Simply because it exists, has momentum, and stopping it would require a conversation nobody wants to have. This is the Preservation Problem in its purest form.
04
What would we not start today, knowing what we now know?
The most important question. The things you would not start are the things that should stop. Not next year. Not after the next planning cycle. The answer to this question is your kill list. It is also your roadmap for reclaiming speed, focus, and resource.

Chapter 10
The LESS Toolkit
Seven biases. Seven interventions. One direction.
Interventions
The framework
LESS is not a philosophy of doing less.
It is the discipline of keeping systems light. Each intervention below maps directly to the bias that generates the tax — and identifies which LESS principle applies. Four letters. One direction. Always subtractive.
L
Lighten
Remove the weight. Light systems move faster, decide better, and last longer. Heaviness is always optional. Applied where accumulated mass is slowing the organisation down.
E
Eliminate
If it doesn't add value, it adds cost. Remove without sentiment. The hardest cuts are always the right ones. Applied where things persist only because they already exist.
S
Simplify
The clearest version is almost always the most effective. Complexity is rarely necessary. Applied where proliferation of options, priorities, or processes has obscured the signal.
S
Sharpen
What remains must be precise. Clarity is earned, not given. Applied where the question being asked is wrong — or is not being asked at all.
