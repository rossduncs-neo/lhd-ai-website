Chapter 00
Before you start
The most common mistake is rushing to create an account before you know what you're writing, for whom, and why.
Foundation
The 30-minute investment
Spend time here. Save months of drift.
Spend 30 minutes on the questions in this chapter before you open Substack. Writers who skip this step spend the first six months of their newsletter trying to answer questions they should have answered before their first post.
Find your niche

Substack rewards specificity. A newsletter about 'business and culture' competes with millions of blogs. A newsletter about 'the business of professional sport in Australia' serves a clear, searchable, recommendable audience.

Your niche test

"I write [what] for [who] so they can [outcome]."

If you can't complete that sentence cleanly, your niche isn't defined yet. Don't proceed until you can.

Your name decision
Use your real name if...
You already have an audience elsewhere — social media, podcasting, journalism.
You are the brand. People follow people.
The newsletter is personal, opinionated, or voice-led.
Coin a publication title if...
The content is bigger than you — a curated digest or collaborative project.
It's a topic-first brand you might eventually hand off or expand.
Either way: short, easy to spell, easy to say aloud. No numbers or hyphens.
Choose your cadence

Readers value consistency far more than frequency. A weekly newsletter delivered every Tuesday at 7am builds more trust than a 'whenever I feel like it' approach.

Daily
Only if your content is genuinely short-form — a chart, a quote, a 200-word take.
Weekly
Sweet spot
The sweet spot for most writers. Consistent, sustainable, expected.
Fortnightly
Perfectly fine, especially for long-form research or analysis.
Monthly
Works for niche deep-dives, but subscriber retention is harder.
Prepare your assets

Have these ready before you open Substack. Gathering them mid-setup creates friction and leads to rushed decisions.

Profile photo — well-lit, clear, at least 400×400px. Not a blurry candid.
Publication logo or wordmark — square format, readable at small sizes.
One-paragraph 'about' blurb — ready to paste into your profile.
First two or three post ideas — written down, so you know you have things to say.
Your niche sentence — completed and tested on someone who doesn't know you.
Chapter 01
Creating your account
The decisions you make during setup are harder to change than they appear. Make them deliberately.
Setup
01
Sign up
Go to substack.com and click 'Start writing'. Register with an email address or via Google/Apple sign-in. Use a professional email address you intend to keep long-term — it appears in your email sends and is harder to change later than you might expect.
02
Claim your subdomain — carefully
Your Substack URL defaults to yourname.substack.com. This is the most important decision you make during setup.
It is very difficult to change later without breaking every link you have ever shared. It appears in every email your subscribers receive. Treat it exactly like registering a domain name — all lowercase, no spaces, something that still makes sense in five years.
You can later connect a custom domain via Settings → Publication → Custom domain, but your subdomain remains your permanent backend address.
03
Verify your email
Check your inbox immediately after signing up and click the verification link. Unverified accounts can experience deliverability problems — your posts may land in spam folders.
04
Connect Stripe for payments
Even if you have no plans to charge readers right now, connect your Stripe account via Settings → Payments. It takes five minutes.
Do this now. It means you can switch on paid subscriptions at any future moment without disrupting your readers or your workflow. Waiting until you're ready to charge creates unnecessary friction at exactly the wrong moment.
Chapter 02
Configuring your publication
Most writers rush through setup. The writers who get this right spend the rest of their time compounding the advantage.
Configuration
01
Write a compelling description
This appears as your SEO snippet, your Substack discovery listing, and the first line potential subscribers read. Two sentences maximum.
Sentence 1: what you publish, specifically. Sentence 2: who it's for, or why it matters. Avoid 'insights', 'musings', 'thoughts on', or 'exploring'. Be concrete. 'Weekly analysis of the Australian property market for first-home buyers' is ten times more compelling than 'Insights on property and finance'.
02
Upload your branding
Go to Settings → Publication → Branding. Upload your logo (256×256px minimum, clear on both light and dark backgrounds) and cover image (1920×1080px recommended).
Avoid stock photography. Original or custom imagery signals authenticity and helps you stand out in Substack's discovery feed.
03
Set up your About page
Go to Posts → Pages → About. This is the page curious readers visit after discovering you. Answer four things:
WhoYour name, relevant background, credibility markers.
WhatWhat you publish — specific, not vague.
WhenYour publishing cadence.
WhyThe promise you make to your readers.
End with a clear call to action: 'Subscribe below to get every issue in your inbox.'
04
Customise your welcome email
Go to Settings → Email → Welcome email. This is statistically your highest-opened email — ever. New subscribers open it at rates of 60–80%, compared to 30–40% for regular posts.
Most writers leave the default 'Thanks for subscribing' template. Don't. Write it like a personal letter. Tell them you're glad they're here, exactly what they'll receive and when, and one thing they can do right now.
05
Configure subscription tiers
Under Settings → Payments, set up free and paid tiers even before you activate charging. Key decisions for Australian writers:
Pricing (AUD)
A$8–15/month or A$80–120/year for standard paid tier. A$150–300/year for founding members. Substack takes 10% plus Stripe fees — around 13–16% of gross earnings total.
Enable AUD pricing
Settings → Payments → turn on localised pricing. Australian readers see prices in AUD rather than USD. Substack supports AUD natively.
Chapter 03
Writing your first post
Your first post is your launch. Treat it like one.
Publishing
01
Start with a welcome post
Before writing substantive content, publish a short public welcome post — think of it as your 'hello world'. Keep it under 500 words. It doesn't need to be your best writing — it needs to be honest and specific about what you're building.
This post lives permanently on your homepage and in your archive. It is linked from your welcome email. It tells the story of why you're starting this newsletter.
02
Learn the editor
Substack's editor is deliberately minimal. Key features to know:
/ — insert a divider, image, video embed, or paywall break
Embeds — paste a Twitter/X, YouTube, or Spotify URL on its own line
Paywall — marks where free readers stop and paid subscribers continue
Headings — render very large in email. Use bold body text instead for most subheadings.
03
Always preview before sending
Use the 'Send test email' button before publishing any post that will send to subscribers. Check it on your phone.
Email clients strip certain formatting, render images at unexpected sizes, and may break layout. Catching this yourself costs nothing. Sending a broken post to your whole list costs trust.
04
When to publish
Research consistently shows that Tuesday through Thursday mornings (in your audience's local timezone) produce the highest open rates. Schedule posts via the dropdown next to the Publish button.
For your very first post: publish, then immediately share it manually on every relevant social channel. The first post is your launch — treat it like one.
Chapter 04
Growing your subscribers
Your existing subscribers are your best salespeople. Build from the inside out.
Growth
01
Import existing contacts
Go to Subscribers → Import. Upload a CSV of email addresses from a prior newsletter, blog mailing list, or LinkedIn connection export. These people already have a relationship with you — they convert at far higher rates than cold traffic.
Only import people who have genuinely opted in to hear from you. Importing random contacts will get you marked as spam and damage your deliverability — possibly permanently.
02
Substack Recommendations — your biggest growth lever
Recommendations (Settings → Recommendations) is currently the most powerful organic growth tool on the platform. When another writer recommends you, their subscribers see your publication suggested at the moment they subscribe.
Recommend other publications you genuinely read and respect. Reach out to the writers — many will recommend you back. Focus on adjacent niches whose audience overlaps with yours. A single recommendation from a publication with 10,000+ subscribers can bring hundreds of new readers.
03
Substack Notes
Notes is Substack's social feed, accessible in the app and on the web. It's discoverable within the Substack ecosystem and is currently under-used by most writers.
Post there 2–3 times a week — short observations, questions, excerpts, reactions. Restacks and comments drive algorithmic visibility inside Substack.
04
The referral programme
Enable it via Settings → Referrals. Reward subscribers who refer friends — with free months, bonus posts, or early access to something.
Your existing subscribers are your best salespeople, and a structured incentive dramatically increases how often they share.
05
Cross-promotion and social
Each time you publish, share it on at least one other platform where your potential audience lives. Consistently linking back to your Substack — not just once at launch — is how you build a pipeline of new readers over months, not days.
Chapter 05
Dos & Don'ts
The rules that separate writers who build something lasting from those who drift.
Reference
Do
Publish on a consistent schedule — even a short post on time beats a brilliant post late.
Reply to every comment in your first few months. Community builds loyalty.
Make your welcome email personal and specific — it's your highest-opened send.
Put your best work in the free tier early on, to attract new readers.
Write subject lines like you're emailing a friend, not writing marketing copy.
Connect Stripe early, even before you plan to charge.
Claim your subdomain as carefully as you'd register a domain name.
Use Recommendations proactively — reach out to writers you respect.
Preview every post in your own inbox before sending.
Avoid
Don't import contacts who never opted in — it will get you marked as spam.
Don't paywall most of your content before you have an established free audience.
Don't write for a vague general audience — niche down as hard as you can.
Don't obsess over subscriber numbers in the first 90 days.
Don't promise a publishing cadence you can't maintain during a busy week.
Don't use a Gmail address as your main contact if you want to look professional.
Don't go paid before you have at least a few hundred engaged free subscribers.
Don't leave the default welcome email — customise it on day one.
Don't skip the About page — it's where curious readers decide whether to subscribe.
The only question that matters
Read your own work as if you've just discovered it via a search.
Know nothing about the author. Would you subscribe? That is the only question that matters at the start.
