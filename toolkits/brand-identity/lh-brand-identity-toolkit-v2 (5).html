01 — Brand Foundation
The philosophy behind the system
LESS Framework · Three Principles
Design Principle
The problem we solve
Complexity is the default state.
Every organisation begins simple. A small team, a clear goal, fast decisions. Then something gets added — a meeting, a process, a tool, a layer. Nothing gets removed.

light house exists for leaders who understand that the most important strategic act is not addition. It is subtraction. Knowing what to remove, having the discipline to remove it, and building the habit of keeping the house light.

The brand identity reflects this exactly. Muted. Warm. Restrained. Every element earns its place or is removed.
The brand principle
This is not a palette.
It is a behavioural system.
Colour signals meaning, not decoration. Tone signals hierarchy, not style. The neutral environment carries the brand values as much as the mark does.

Colour = meaning.
Tone = hierarchy.
Neutral = environment.

Every design decision is a subtraction decision. If it doesn't add meaning, it is removed.
The LESS Framework
L
Lighten
Remove the weight. Light systems move faster, decide better, and last longer. Heaviness is always optional.
E
Eliminate
If it doesn't add value, it adds cost. Remove without sentiment. The hardest cuts are always the right ones.
S
Simplify
The clearest version of a thing is almost always the most effective. Complexity is rarely necessary.
S
Sharpen
What remains must be precise. Clarity is earned, not given. Sharpen until the signal becomes obvious.
The Three Principles
First Principle
Subtraction
Complexity is the default state. Systems naturally grow heavier over time. The discipline of removing what is unnecessary is not a one-time act — it is a habit.
Second Principle
Lightness
Light systems move faster, adapt more easily, and consume less energy. Lightness is not aesthetic minimalism — it is functional simplicity. Optimal structures do more with less.
Third Principle
Clarity
What isn't obvious cannot be executed. Visibility is the prerequisite for action. Every strategy should be transparent and immediately intuitive. If it needs explaining, it needs simplifying.
The design principle — in one sentence
"Muted, warm, editorial system with semantic colour coding — where colour signals meaning, not decoration."

The tonal rule
Always three tones
Every semantic colour appears in three forms. This is not optional — it is the system. Light for backgrounds. Mid for borders. Base for text and accents.
Why it works
Systematic pairing
A card using teal always uses teal-light background, teal-mid border, and teal text. The three tones always travel together. This creates visual coherence without effort.
The hidden rule
Everything is muted
No pure saturation anywhere in the system. This gives the brand authority, editorial tone, and a "thinking product" feel. Muted is not weak — it is deliberate.

06 — Design Principles
Why the system works
Three principles · One voice
Principle 01
Everything is slightly muted
No pure saturation anywhere in the system. Every colour is desaturated enough to feel considered rather than corporate. This gives the brand authority, editorial tone, and a "thinking product" feel. Muted is not timid — it is deliberate.
Teal is #5DB5A3, not #00BFA5 · Gold is #C8A96E, not #FFC107 · Red is #B5453A, not #F44336
Principle 02
Warm bias across all neutrals
Even the greys are slightly brown. Ink is not pure black (#000) — it is warm near-black (#18160F). Paper is not pure white (#FFF) — it is warm cream (#F6F3EE). This warm bias creates cohesion with the gold and avoids the sterility of a tech aesthetic.
Compare #18160F (our ink) vs #000000 (pure black) — the difference is warmth
Principle 03
Colour signals meaning, not decoration
This is the most important principle. Teal means insight. Gold means action. Red means caution. Violet means evidence. These assignments are not aesthetic choices — they are semantic decisions. Once established, they must be applied consistently or the system loses its value.
If teal appears for decoration, it stops working as a signal. Every use must be intentional.
The hidden layer — what makes the system sophisticated
Colour
= Meaning
Each colour carries one semantic role. Used consistently across every touchpoint. Colour teaches without needing explanation.
Tone
= Hierarchy
The three tones (light/mid/base) signal importance within each colour. Light is background. Mid is structure. Base is foreground emphasis.
Neutral
= Environment
The warm ink and paper scales create the environment. They carry the brand values as much as the mark does. Space is not empty — it is working.
07 — Usage Rules
Do this. Not that.
Clear permissions and prohibitions
Do
✓
Use the mark at its correct proportions. The 70 × 90 viewbox ratio is fixed. Scale uniformly in all directions.
✓
Maintain the gap between the two bars. The space is structural, not decorative. It is the idea made visible.
✓
Set "light" in ExtraLight 200 and "house" in Bold 700. The weight contrast is the wordmark. Never equalise the weights.
✓
Use the tonal trio consistently. When a semantic colour appears, use all three tones together: light background, mid border, base text.
✓
Apply colours semantically. Teal = insight. Gold = action. Red = caution. Violet = evidence. Always.
✓
Use generous white space. Minimum clear space around the lockup equals the height of the LH Mark. The space is brand.
✓
Keep everything slightly muted. No pure saturation. The system is built on deliberate restraint.
Do not
✗
Never stretch, rotate, or distort the LH Mark. The proportional relationship between the bars is non-negotiable.
✗
Never fill both bars with the signal colour. The thin bar takes the accent colour only. The thick bar remains ink or warm white.
✗
Never set "light" and "house" at the same weight. The tension between them is the point. Equalising them destroys the mark.
✗
Never use colour decoratively. If you cannot explain why a colour is used semantically, remove it. Decoration undermines the signal system.
✗
Never use typefaces other than Outfit and DM Mono. No exceptions. Both are free on Google Fonts.
✗
Never place the mark on a busy or clashing background. The approved grounds are: warm white, paper, ink. Signal accent version on dark only.
✗
Never use pure white (#FFFFFF) or pure black (#000000) as primary surfaces. The warm neutrals are essential to the brand character.
